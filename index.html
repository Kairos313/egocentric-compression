The Problem

Upload is the bottleneck for Physical AI data scaling.

A data collection device produces 10 GB per worker-day. 1,000 devices in a single factory = 10 TB/day to upload. A typical industrial ISP in India provisions 500 Mbps of shared upload. Under these conditions, the minimum cycle time per factory is 44 hours — longer than a day.

The current product format (456×256 at 30fps H.265) produces ~2 GB per 8-hour shift. For 1,000 devices, that's 2 TB/day — requiring 9.2 hours to upload over 500 Mbps.

An egocentric-native codec should exploit the unique properties of head-mounted factory video: fixed environment, repetitive tasks, camera on a human head. The goal is to reduce upload size while preserving quality for Physical AI training.

What We Tried (15 Approaches)

Exhaustive testing revealed that H.265 is already extremely optimized internally. Almost every external preprocessing approach duplicates what the codec already does.

What Works

WORKS
Pixel Binning (2x2 / 4x4 area averaging)
The only approach that meaningfully outperforms H.265 alone. Reduces pixel count before encoding — the one thing the codec can't do internally. 2x2 binning at CRF 28 achieves 2.5x compression; 4x4 at CRF 23 achieves 4.9x. 10-bit encoding preserves fractional bin averages.

What Doesn't Work (and Why)

FAILS
Neural Super-Resolution (ESRGAN, SwinIR)
+0.21 dB over Lanczos at best. Pretrained SR models expect clean bicubic degradation, not H.265 compression artifacts (blocking, ringing, banding). The models hallucinate textures that don't match the original, hurting SSIM. Tested both pretrained and fine-tuned on 2,000 egocentric frame pairs. SwinIR was actually worse than Lanczos.
FAILS
IMU Motion Prediction
Gyroscope explains only 1% of pixel motion. 72% of motion is local (hands/tools/objects) — IMU has zero information about this. 27% is head translation parallax — gyroscope can't predict depth-dependent motion. H.265 motion vectors already handle the 1% rotation component.
FAILS
ROI QP Maps (Zipstream-style)
43% LARGER than uniform CRF at same quality. H.265 already does per-CTU adaptive QP internally. Splitting into two streams (high-quality ROI + low-quality background) loses inter-region temporal coherence. Works for fixed surveillance cameras, not head-mounted cameras with constant global motion.
FAILS
Background Prior Compositing
Camera is head-mounted and moves constantly. 66% of pixels are classified as "foreground" because the viewpoint changes every frame. Cross-clip background SSIM is 0.13 — the worker moves to completely different areas. A static background reference is useless.
FAILS
Chroma Separation (Luma 30fps + Color 2fps)
Combined streams are LARGER than full-color encoding. H.265 YUV 4:2:0 already subsamples chroma 4x. A separate low-fps color stream adds I-frame overhead that exceeds any savings.
FAILS
Per-Shot Adaptive CRF
3-6% savings with 3.8 dB quality loss. Each segment boundary requires a costly I-frame that destroys inter-segment temporal redundancy. H.265 already adapts internally via skip blocks — explicitly varying CRF adds overhead without meaningful savings.
FAILS
Global Motion Compensation (ORB + Homography)
Encoding residuals is 12% LARGER than raw H.265. The residual signal (centered at 128) looks like noise to the codec, defeating temporal prediction. Reconstruction errors compound frame-over-frame. H.265's internal motion compensation handles global motion better.
MARGINAL
Temporal Noise Reduction (hqdn3d)
2.8% bitrate saving, 0.008 SSIM loss. Source is already H.265 compressed — no temporal noise left to remove. Could be added as a free preprocessing step but not a priority.
MARGINAL
Adaptive GOP Length
1.3% saving (keyint 250 → 5400). At 456x256, I-frames are tiny. CRF selection has 36x more impact than GOP tuning.
MARGINAL
IMU Adaptive Frame Dropping
17% of frames droppable at 0.1 rad/s threshold, but "stationary" frames still show 5.4% pixel change from hand motion. H.265 skip blocks already handle temporal redundancy. Not worth ARM implementation complexity.
FAILS
VQ-VAE Patch Codebook
17.9 dB reconstruction quality — too low. Patch-level quantization loses spatial context.
FAILS
Cycle-Aware Compression via IMU
Workers are not performing identical motions at the IMU signal level. Autocorrelation confidence <0.3. Repetition exists at a semantic level, not at the pixel/motion level.

Why Hardware Limits Compression

The fundamental constraint is the device hardware. The Build AI Gen 4 device has:

ARM Cortex-A76 (quad-core) + H.265 Hardware Encoder

No GPU. No NPU (Neural Processing Unit). The only video encoding available is the fixed H.265 hardware block.

This means:

What the device CAN doWhat the device CANNOT do
Downsample / bin pixels (trivial math)Run neural networks (no GPU/NPU)
Drop framesLearned compression (DCVC-RT needs A100 GPU)
Set H.265 encoder parameters (CRF, profile)Content-aware encoding (needs real-time inference)
Simple pixel operations (area averaging)Feature extraction, object detection

Every neural codec paper (DCVC-RT, AutoGaze, OneVision) assumes encoder-side intelligence — GPU-class compute for learned transforms. With a fixed H.265 hardware encoder, these approaches are inaccessible.

H.265 is already extremely efficient at what it does internally: motion compensation, adaptive QP per coding tree unit, temporal skip blocks, entropy coding. 14 of our 15 approaches failed because they tried to duplicate what the codec already handles.

The only lever external to the codec is reducing the pixel count before encoding — which is why pixel binning is the single approach that works.

Path Forward: Gen 5 with NPU

Adding a Neural Processing Unit (4+ TOPS) to future devices would unlock learned compression:
